March 14/96 Dear Mr. Gere1;

I inclose a letter from Professor Adams3 which will explain its self. I have just seen regent C. W. Kaley4 and he tells me that the whole thing business of appointing the new Instructor5 rests with Mr. Morrill6, himself and the Chancellor7. I think if I could get the appointment for the remaining two months of the year that I could hold it next year. I have been in all of Bate's8 class classes and he writes that he will recommend me to any extent I desire. If you could see the Chancellor and Mr. Morrill for me I think my chances would be good. Of course the two principal things against me are my age and sex,9 but I think I could over come both of those. On the other hand the university is hard pressed for funds and I would go in as an Instructor and they would pay me five hundred dollars less than the man they intended getting who would have been Adjunct Professor. Mr. Bates says that whatever is done must be done at once, and untill I can get up to Lincoln10 I must trust to my friends. If you could see Mr. Morrill and the Chancellor it would be a great help. Please ask Mr. Jones11 to send me some transportation12 as soon as possible so that I can go up to see to it. I telegraphed him please to send it this afternoon, but he may not understand.

Of course you know how much the appointment would mean to me if I could only get it. I have had some experience in coaching students in English and I am sure I could teach it.

Faithfully yours Willa Cather.

Person Annotations

0046

Gere, Charles Henry (1838-1904). Newspaper publisher; Cather's employer during college. Charles H. Gere, born in New York, graduated from Dickinson College in Carlisle, PA, in 1861 and joined the Union army. In 1865 he moved to Nebraska, where his family had moved. Politically active in the Republican Party, he moved to Lincoln, NE, and founded the city’s first newspaper, the Nebraska State Journal. He married Mariel E. Clapham of Washington, D.C., in 1871. While serving as a representative in the first state legislature, he helped found the University of Nebraska, for which he served as regent from 1882 to 1892. Cather wrote for his newspaper when she was a university student, and became a friend of Charles and Mariel Gere’s three daughters, Mariel, Ellen, and Frances. She remembered Charles Gere’s “kindness, his easy wit, the ease and charm of his personality.”

0906

Adams, James Ware. American educator and farmer. After earning his Bachelor of Letters from the University of Michigan in 1889, James W. Adams taught English at the University of Nebraska in Lincoln from 1892-1897, where he knew Cather as a student and was a fellow member of the English Club. He appears to have left teaching in 1897 to return to his hometown of Normal, IL, to be a fruit grower.

0534

Kaley, Charles W. (1846-1917). Red Cloud lawyer and University of Nebraska regent. Born in Pennsylvania, Kaley lived in Ohio before coming to Red Cloud, NE, in 1874. He was admitted to the bar in 1876 and was, for many years, the local representative for the Burlington Railroad’s land office; he retired in 1896. He served on the state board of education and was a regent of the University of Nebraska from 1894 to 1898. Cather appealed to him in 1896 when she applied for a teaching position at the university. Kaley and his wife, Cyrelia Widney Kaley, had no children, but several brothers, an uncle, and cousins lived in Red Cloud at various times; one of his sisters, Anna, married Howard Cather, Charles F. Cather’s cousin.

1031

Morrill, Charles Henry (1842-1928). Businessman, University of Nebraska regent, and fossil collector. Born in New Hampshire, C. H. Morrill fought in the Civil War with the 11th New Hampshire Volunteers. After the war, he moved west, eventually settling near Stromsburg, NE, where he was successful in banking. In 1890, Morrill was elected to the Board of Regents of the University of Nebraska; soon after he and his family moved to Lincoln, NE, where he grew increasingly active in the Republican party. He was the elected President of the Board from 1893 to 1903. In the 1890s he began the Morrill Geological Expedition to gather fossils, primarily in western Nebraska. A major financial benefactor to the University, he also donated his fossil collection to its museum. When a new museum opened in 1927 it was named Morrill Hall in his honor.

0999

McLean, George Edwin (1850-1938). American academic and university administrator. Born in Rockville, CT, George Edwin McLean received his Bachelor of Divinity from Yale Divinity School (1874), his PhD from Leipzig University (1883), and his Doctor of Laws from Williams College (1895). In 1895, when James Canfield recommended him for the position of chancellor at the University of Nebraska, he was an English professor at the University of Minnesota. He was chancellor when Willa Cather applied for a teaching position at the University of Nebraska in 1896. In 1899 he left to become president of the State University of Iowa.

0462

Bates, Herbert (1868-1929). American educator and writer. Born in Massachusetts, Bates received his B.A. from Harvard (1890), where he contributed to campus publications. In 1891 he came to the University of Nebraska as an instructor in rhetoric; Cather was one of his students, and he inspired the character of Gaston Cleric in My Ántonia (1918). When he left Nebraska in 1896 she applied unsuccessfully for his position. He published a volume of poetry, Songs of Exile (1896), before joining the faculty of the Manual Training High School in Brooklyn, NY, in 1897. He became active in the National Council of Teachers of English, wrote A History of English Literature (1918), and edited Modern Lyric Poetry (1929), and various classics. Cather owned a copy of his edition of Coleridge’s The Rime of the Ancient Mariner (1895) and kept in occasional touch with him until his death.

0069

Jones, Will Owen (1862-1928). Newspaper editor. Will Owen Jones was born in Wisconsin and came to Nebraska by 1880. He worked for the Nebraska State Journal while attending the University of Nebraska in Lincoln, graduating in 1886. That year he became city editor of the paper, then associate editor in 1889 (the year he married pianist Edith M. Doolittle), and then managing editor in 1892. He was close to Charles and Mariel Gere, the owners of the Nebraska State Journal, and the Jones’s only child, Mariel Jones, was named for Mrs. Gere. Cather took his journalism course at the university in 1893 and wrote to him often early in her career, although he could be severe in his criticism of some of her work.
